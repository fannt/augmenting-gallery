mediations and education

mediation and social distinctions

the modern museum is stretched between an aesthetic, ethic and social role.

but how does one build the bridge between seeing and knowing?

Mediation means meeting someone in the middle, of having to concile (or re-concile!) two or more different parties. What is reconciled here is the truth held by museums, as the cultural heritage which needs to be transmitted to the public in order to reinforce the sense of unity and communal belonging of this public. Additionally, mediation and education are rooted in the enlightenment spirit of learning and developing knowledge.

But if mediation—and its counterpart, interpretation—imply closing the gap on the distance between the artwork and the public, woulnd't be interesting to know why that gap is here in the first place?

hermeneutics

the methodology of interpreting sacred texts reinforces the sacredness of these texts.

Museums have, for a long time, never questioned how to proceed with education. It has always been top-down, explanatory, or even ommitted. Mediation has indeed been activated once the audience is in the museum, and not so much before such audiences would come to the museum.

The approach is therefore self-contained and self-referential: it only exists within the world of the museum, since the museum represents the world, and it explains the artworks only in relationship to the artworks themselves. Art for art's sake is the manifestation of the luxury/privilege.

taste as distinction

"museums exists so that people who go to museums can distinguish themselves from those who do not go" - pierre bourdieu

social capital <--> economic capital

economic capital <- -> cultural capital

cultural capital <- -> social capital

a need for new types of education

didactic learning implies a reinforcing of implicit power dynamics and capital inequalities.

are there other forms of learning which address these issues?


educational approaches

debates about learning

education is the action of developing moral, physical, intellectual and scientific values, skills and knowledge

classical approach

the teacher provides knowledge to the learner by feeding them truths/facts that already exist about the world.

constructivism

any experience which allows for subsequent, novel experiences is a learning experience

Constructivism has a particular appeal to the museum, because it is suited to informal, voluntary, one-shot nature of most learning which happens in a museum.

First developed by John Dewey, political philosopher and educator, constructivism challenges the passive learner approach and offers activities to the learner so that they can construct knowledge by themselves.

the ignorant schoolmaster

"because something has been learned by someone, and because we are all born equal, then that thing can be learned by anyone" - jacques rancière, the ignorant schoolmaster

-> how can museums act as ignorant structures?

teaching to transgress

the most important question in education is:

why should one learn this?

once this question is answered, the following is:

how should one learn this?

All of the above are part of a broader field named critical pedagogies, including educators such as paulo freire, who brings in ideas about narration and storytelling as essential to learning and bell hooks, who argues that a central element of teaching is the ability for the teacher to encourage and motivate individuals into a learning situation through performance (i.e. not just reading notes from a pedestal in an amphitheater).


museum initiatives

museum responses

while the formats remain the same, the means to convey those formats have evolved.

  • paper
  • audio
  • tours
  • workshops
  • collaborations

Museums have educational activities through:

  • information labels
  • gallery tours
  • informal gallery presence
  • class visits
  • family programs
  • organizational partnerships
  • online educational programs

Amongst these, the first ones on the list (labels and tours) are more didactic and unidirectional, while the others can be developed into more participative and horizontal practices.

The later items (family activities, online programs) present the added complication that learning in museums is conducted in conditions ill-suited to traditional learning: short duration, sporadic, in unfamiliar settings and unrelated to existing disciplined educational exposure. This, however, does make them more conducive to a constructivist approach to education.

collaborating physically

physical activities can be the starting point for any sort of learning experience.

Museums invite more and more artist collectives to create experiences in their physical spaces, which involve the audience. The TLC collective in Mexico City develops activities with the museum visitors, and start by asking questions in order to understand what is it that is interesting to them.

Complementary to this face-to-face approach, the architecture agency raumlabor has partnered with the staatliches museen zu berlin in order to install interactive installations which involve the visitors further with the artworks they are currently seeing in the room. (full 360 visit here)

collaborating digitally

how can digital media can contribute to modifying/muting the curatorial voice?

For instance, adding comments on an artwork, even though no official app of no big museum is offering this.

Offering one's own recombining threads of narrative beyond chronological/geographical. There is no way to share the pathway that one has gone through to other future visitors. Such a sharing feature would allow a different perspective on the authority of curation.

augmented reality and education

existing AR apps unveil existing content, rather than allowing visitors to create it themselves (e.g. X-Ray vision).
