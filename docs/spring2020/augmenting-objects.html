defining culture

the set of visible and invisible relations between individuals of a similar group, as expressed through possessions, actions, location, speech, etc., in order to create and sustain a shared meaning

aka practicing a collective dream

Culture can be approached in multiple ways: culture as the coherent element of a group ("youth culture") but also as the defining markers between different groups ("high-brow" vs. "low-brow" cultures).

As the object of study of fields like anthropology (study of humans and human behavior, in particular through their meaning-making practices) or sociology (study of the patterns of social interactions), culture is diffused across all components of a social group, and therefore more elusive than the specific representations of, say, religion, politics or the arts. Culture is the web which underpins such other fields of action.

Knowing about culture allows us to know more about ourselves; digital media, as information delivery, can help us achieve that goal.

cultural vs. natural

what is the difference between something artificial and something natural?

The difference is, no pun intended, artificial. It can be argued that everything that humans touch become artificial (including dog breeds, forests, mountains), while the denomination of "the natural" is a label applied to certain things which we imagine to be normal, untouched, like a "natural" park.

The point of approaching our environment as artificial (i.e. human-made, or human-modified) is that it unlocks a set of questions as to why humans have created, or appropriated this particular object. What makes it important? What does it represent for the group of humans concerned with it? On how many different "levels" does it operate?

cultural studies

cultural studies is a field that studies cultural production and consumption in order to study the systems of power within a given group of people.

it is important to: - understand what a culture is in order to convince and persuade them of things - understand the frameworks that we are operating in (remember the arbitrariness of things)

Cultural studies doesn't just look at culture just to explore what is going on (descriptive) but to give reasons as to why that must be going on (explicative). What do cultural practices (i.e. practices of meaning-making between humans) tell us about how those humans are organized? About what matters to them? About how they see the world.

Particularly, cultural studies scholars such as Stuart Hall and Raymond Williams were interested in the class structures and systems of power as they manifest themselves not through explicit modes of constraint (like Foucault's investigation of disciplinary systems), or through quantitative methods (like Bourdieu's field study techniques), but through implicit, yet evocative practices. For example, how does how one set the table represent one's socio-economic belonging, or aspirations? How does one's leisure activities act as someone's desire to fit within a group? How could those activities be imposed, to an extent, upon those who practice them?

museums as cultural institutions

so what kind of culture do museums present?

Museums tend to develop both for cultural practices and as cultural practices. On the one hand, museums document, present and highlight aspects of different cultures (across time and/or space), and therefore have a responsibility in conveying a "truth" about the collections and the objects they are presenting.

On the other hand, however, they also act as culture, and in that they are the object of a cultural practice (e.g. going to the museum on sundays). In that sense, the museums themselves become representative of our culture.


objects

approaches

what is an object?

an object is a material creation whose form and/or function support human living on multiple levels

Objects are first and foremost materials: they can be seen, touched, heard, smelled, before they can be understood. Through this materiality, objects embody more invisible, evanescent, sometimes "ideological" relationships and therefore act as testimonies to our culture.

Objects can have a range of sizes, from a toothpick to the sixth continent of plastic (often qualifying as hyperobjects)

objects and culture

"artefacts are a means by which we give form to, and come to an understanding of, ourselves, others" -daniel miller

Daniel Miller is an anthropologist, and aims at highlighting the need for all societies to look at their objects as essential cultural artefacts, playing complex roles of function and signification between all of us; as a result, he doubts the usefulness of making a clear disctinction between humans and objects. More recently, he's been leading a research project on digital anthropology: why we post

Particularly, artefacts (as Miller calls them) are means of representing not just individuals (through their choice of using those) but mostly as manifestations of the historical and geographical contexts in which they operate. This isn't just limited to the, say, masks of "primitive" populations studied by anthropologists such as a Marcel Mauss and Levi-Strauss, but to all objects created by any society, including (and especially!) societies from which anthropologists typically come from.

ordering

the way we classify things gives them a particular kind of meaning.

structuralism is an approach in which all things, peoples and relationships happen within one defined overarching structure, a cultural project with explicit rules.

being ordered

on the other hand, being ordered means that objects act as a classification systems for humans.

essentially, objects help humans represent themselves.

creating meaning

how does the walkman create meaning?

three moments in meaning-making:

  • production (where and when was it made? by whom?)
  • mediation (how does it present itself to its audience? what words and images are being used to represent it?)
  • consumption (how is it actually used? how is that use congruent with the intended use?)

There are actually a couple of other moments, according to Paul DuGay and his co-authors of Doing Cultural Studies. These include:

  • representation (how is the object shown to us? both by its creators, and by others?)
  • identity (what are the traits that are associated with the object, beyond what the creators want to have explicitly presented?)
  • production (in a very marxist sense: what are the practical conditions of the creation of the object, and how do those relate to broader ideological conditions?)
  • consumption (how do people actually engage with it?)
  • regulation (under what circumstances is the object allowed or not? by which legal/moral system?)

the boundary between humans and objects

there doesn't need to be a particular difference between objects and humans, since objects can have personality, and humans can act as objects.

this idea is explored further in actor-network theory.


techniques of AR

recognizing objects

the problem of AR is to make a correlation between the 3D (physical) world and the 3D (digital) world.

the first and easiest way to do so was to use markers as a connection point, and then calculate the difference between the original image and the calculated image.

(today, 3D objects can be used as markers as well, but you still need to scan them first)

recognizing spaces

this is called markerless AR, because it doesn't need a specific image, but instead uses a GPS, gyroscope and software to deduce the position, scale and orientation of what is being looked at.

arkit, arcore and arfoundation

ARKit is developed by Apple, has more advanced features, but targets less devices.

ARCore, made by Google, still catches up to apple's features, but addresses a lot more devices.

ARFoundation is Unity's answer to both, allowing us to develop in one framework, and indiscriminately target ARKit or ARCore

finally, Vuforia is the "ancestor" of contemporary AR, having been around for a bit longer, and a lot more reliable/stable, while not offering all the features of ARKit or ARCore.

All of the above rely on a field of computer science called Computer Vision (a series of math operations being applied to an image in order to extract its features), and particularly aim at recognizing feature points, which are points in an image which can be kept track of, in order to build up spatial awareness of what is being looked at. These feature points are essential, and this is why you might not see good AR results if you are only, say, looking at a dark, flat surface with no reflections (e.g. the void)

How these feature points are implemented differ slightly between libraries and software solutions, mostly based on funding of the teams and market demand: in the end, they all do the same thing!

implementation in unity

any unity scene with AR capabilities needs three objects: Session, Session Origin and AR Camera.

ARSessionOrigin, with an AR Camera as a child, takes care of calculating where Unity is in the real world, and adjusting the virtual camera to match that view.

ARSession has the responsibility to start, save, restart, recall and close AR Sessions. it takes care of a lot of the nitty gritty that we don't particularly want to deal with.

visualizing and interacting in unity

To visualize the world as it is recognized by the ARFoundation framework, we need to add a AR [xxxx] Manager, (e.g. a AR Plane Manager), which will display all planes recognized by Unity, by instantiating a prefab at the detected location.

To interact with the world, we need to use the special Racyast manager provided by ARFoundation, and which is located on the ARSession component. Which results in a code like:

GameObject go = GameObject.Find("AR Session");
go.GetComponent<ARSession>().Raycast(Input.GetTouch(0).position, etc.);

unity

unity review

Raycasting needs:

  • an origin point
  • a direction
  • a variable to store outputs

From these three elements, raycasting allows us to interact with any objects that have Colliders on them.

colliders

A Collider is a component that we can add to a GameObject in order to make it interactable through physics.

Colliders can act as physical boundaries (bumping into other objects) or as triggers (activating something when a physical event happens, such as walking into a zone).

In order to register those, one of the two colliding objects need to have a Rigidbody.

creating a game object

In order to create a GameObject while the app is running, we can use the following:

//-- we need to have an object, a position and a rotation
public GameObject go;
public Vector3 position;
Quaternion rotation = Quaternion.Identity;

//-- when we create a gameobject, we can store it in a variable
//-- and then instantiate it further
GameObject created = GameObject.Instantiate(go, position, rotation);
//-- here we make it twice as big
created.GetComponent<Transform>().localScale *= 2;

prefabs

Prefabs are a way to save and recall GameObjects.

In order to create a prefab, create a new GameObject in the Hierarchy, and then drag it into the Assets panel. You will see it become blue in the Hierarchy, and you can now create as many copies as you want of this Prefab.

This is particularly useful when you want to instantiate objects at runtime, without having them in your scene to start with.
