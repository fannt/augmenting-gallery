what's wrong with making copies?

before, it used to depreciate the value of a work based on specific workshops.

today, it depreciates the value of a work based on names and tradition.

Making copies has been frowned upon in Western art history ever since the figure of the artist as solitary creator has emerged. This appearance is marked in part by Giorgio Vasari's opus: The Lives of the Artists, in which he depicts the biographies of the artists of his time, essentially giving them a specific status. That status was based on "uniqueness", as opposed to only "skill".

Incidentally, Vasari is also the one who orchestrated the construction and design of the Uffizi palaces, one of the first modern museums. What happened then, is the separation of the artist from the artesan: the former working on unique pieces, while the latter was working on generic designs. The mastery of skill, along with new storytellers like Vasari led to the emergence of the artist as a lone genius. Created by such an artist, the artwork also acquires unique aspects.

Today, museums are built around the core idea of preservation: that there is one original which needs to be safeguarded, because it has lived through history, and perhaps bears its marks. The assumption is that, without this original component of tradition, there would be no following pieces, and no tradition.

the age of reproduction

this question acquires a new dimension in the context of chemical reproduction (photo, film), and then digital reproduction.

walter benjamin wrote about the relationship of aura, tradition and politics.

  • the aestheticization of politics - fascist
  • the politicization of aesthetics - communist

Benjamin talks about the change in our conception of the art object that is brought about by reproduction technologies. Considering the relationship between the original and the copy, he assigns to the original the aura, the sum of historical events that have left a mark on the materiality of that object. This is what copies do not and will never possess. However, he also considers the aura as a means to sustain a fascination for the past/tradition. In that sense, he considers such a reverence for the original object a potential pathway to fascist behaviour (the text itself was written in 1934 as an address to the Institute for the Study of Fascism).

His answer is to offer reproduction as a means to include the broadest public in the appreciation and appropriation of art. He suggests that distributing copies, and allowing any individual to be featured in reproducible materials (i.e. amateur photographs and amateur films), is a necessity for anti-fascistic democratic behavior.

exit through the gift shop

the gift shop (and reproduction in general), can prevent artworks from decaying.

Activating an artwork is an integral part of what the work is actually about, which means that the aura of the original has to be intepreted as a complex web of historical dynamics, rather than as a simple, ephemeral object. The personal interpretation and appropriation of an artwork is what he calls implementation, and it is essential to the survival of said artwork.

conclusion

digital reproduction in the museum exacerbates the tension between the original and the copy, between the proof and the memory.

how can museum still provide the quality traditionally associated with uniqueness in the age of infinite digital reproduction?

The original is still one of the best proofs of historical events, while reproduction is necessary both for democratic ideals and the survival of said works. On the other hand, reproducing works allows for memories of the visitor to be grounded, and last longer.

digital collections

getting online

in the mid-2000s, museums embraced the web as a possibility for expanding their reach, in the midst of the web 2.0, and everything became a blog

two problems, though:

-> inaccuracy of digital reproduction

-> unequal technical skills reveal a lack of clear intent

There were high hopes from the use of online technology for museums. At the same time, the Web 2.0 had started to appear: that is, a web that is no longer static, but collaborative, in which anyone can create, post, and share (e.g. MySpace, Tumblr). Therefore, the visitors started to have different expectations of museums websites and the museums themselves offered solutions.

One of the first problem they encountered was that the digital copies, improved through 2D or 3D rendering, developed a false sense of what was actually scientifcally known (e.g. reconstruction of pieces, close-ups of materials, etc.). It was therefore morally dubious to present these as artefacts from which research could be based on, instead of the originals.

The second problem was that they realized that only a small fraction of their public actually had the skills necessary (both technically and conceptually) to take full advantage of the resources shared by the museum. It was therefore necessary to develop different approaches to a purely scientific database.

architecture

online spaces are architectural spaces, and science and technology studies aim at highlighting the biases and intents that might be embedded in the technical infrastructure.

People make technical choices, and these technical choices impact the final product of this development process. Additionally, they allow us to highlight some of the assumptions built into this technology in order to better understand what this infrastructure was made for in the first place, and what kind of worldview it implicitly conveys.

For instance, The Sims 3 has an architecture that is built around the programming paradigm of Object-Orientation. This means that the true actors in the game are not the Sims themselves, but rather the objects that surround the sims. The bed, the bathroom and the TV all broadcast invisible signals to compete for the attention of the passive Sims. Such an architecture reveals the highly materialistic value set of the game series. Is being online more freeing, or less freeing? When is it either? Does it need to try to reproduce something that existed previously, or does it re-appropriate it to update it to serve its own needs?

On the one hand, this example from the British Museum in 2011 shows how the online component of museums highlights the sense of "placelessness" of modern museum. Similarly to science labs, it advertises "the universality and authority of the culture that builds and inhabits them".

On the other hand, by building upon the existing commercial infrastructure of web platforms and webshops, the museum cannot avoid being presented, to some extent, as a marketplace, failing to convey the idea of the art catalogue as opposed to the sales catalogue.

the museum as street view

some exhibits (thyssen museum) try to mimick a virtual visit, and put the physical back on the center of the stage.

Another approach from the catalogue one is to try to render as straightforwardly as possible. That is, re-creating the visitor experience in a browser, by using techniques such as 360 photo, video, and photogrammetry. While it might seem straightforward, such an approach might also have some heavy influences, including:

  • the "on-rails" aspect the experience (following a set path)
  • the limited embodiment of "one-perspective"
  • the (current) impossibility of a shared social experience

the museum as digital first

taking advantage of tech and design know-how, museums now adapt their shows to the online format (Uffizi) or create online-only shows (Brooklyn Visual Heritage, Europeana, New Palmyra).

-> understanding the frameworks for browsing (mobile)

-> relying on and promoting the use of interconnectivity (APIs, hyperlinks)

Acknowledging the different nature of digital as a medium, museal institutions have approached the problem by starting with the medium, highlighting the **contributive aspect** (New Palmyra and the crowdsourcing of disappeared monuments), the **device-specific** aspect (Uffizi and their mobile navigation) and finally the **connection** aspect (BVH, Europeana). The important points here is to understand that an online presence: - comes with a set of expectations on how to interact with it - comes with a distinct visual language, to which the archival language is not always suited - comes with a whole range of possibilities by interconnecting systems Regarding the last point, APIs are a digital protocol which allows two systems to interface (essentially by agreeing on communication). This allows different servers to receive and broadcast information, potentially ending up in aggregating content from different providers, such as the BVH, or by acting as a source of content, such as Europeana.

conclusion

Museums have operated a slow switch from a blunt dump of information/basic visiting information to the attempt to recreate and enhance the museum visit.

20 years later, we also have online monitoring to best track the behaviour of visitors.


visitor practices

visitor practices

do people act the same way in a physical and an online museum?

Two new elements are useful to get a more fine-grained understanding of what users actually do on museum websites, data analytics and online questionnaires. Both of these techniques have been deployed throughout more and more institutions, and are starting to inform digital decisions of museums. Amongst some of the results, professionals have discovered that they have three different kinds of users:

  1. professional (skillset, knowledge, professional reasons) (25%)
  2. hobbyist (skillset, knowledge, personal reasons) (25%)
  3. novice (no skillset, personal reasons) (50%)

specialized publics

only 3% of users regularly used a museum's collection; it's a highly restricted, highly skilled group of professionals and hobbyists.

most museums have developed their own structures (e.g. based on shared protocols) to accomodate these publics and their creations of folksonomies.

While the professionals know what they want, and know how to get it, hobbyists also represent an essential pool of resources. By tagging and commenting on artworks, they can improve the quality of the archives, through a process called folksonomy, essentially combining the floating-ness of folk knowledge with the rigor of scientific taxonomies.

Integrating those hobbyists illustrate the benefit of having an open collection (open in the technical and practical sense).

the vast majority

doesn't spend too much time on the museum website, and mostly uses it to prepare upcoming visits.

museums (like the rijks) have started to integrate tour-preparation in their digital offering.

In preparing visits, the museums can re-implement the dynamic of the collection. They offer visitors to create their collection before visiting the museum, or to tag their favorite objects in order to consult their collection once they get home, share it with others, etc. This creates a cyclical relationship between offline and online.

responses by museums

while experimenting with different types of information architecture, museums have been confronted with the reality of visitor practices.

they need to adapt to the case of unskilled visitors.

As a result, museums are focusing on the user first, and the collection second, and on how they can best use the strong components of encountering and serendipity. helping with that are (a) photographs with context, (b) references between related objects and (c) different means of browsing, rather than an opaque keyword-base interface.


today

reaching online

how can museums reach the vast majority of their potential audiences?

Indeed, the point isn't to provide the raw materials for education, but to reach out and highlight why such an education is desirable. One of the latest and most massive shift has been the turn away from intrinsic communication (from the museum world to the museum-goers) and towards extrinsic communication (from the museum towards the rest of the world).

The immediate consequence of that is the entrance of museums into the world of social media; they started creating youtube channels, instagram accounts, twitter hashtags, etc. in order to reach where their audience already is, and in a language they can talk to.

the biggest barrier to entry is still the lack of familiarity with the search process, both technical (how?) and conceptual (why?).

the museum as platform

almost all museums have moved on to other platforms (twitter, instagram, facebook, pinterest, etc.)

the inverse phenomenon is happening with google arts and culture.

Museums want to reach the visitors directly in their "natural habitat", so they've started adapting content to fit the media consumption habits of users on social media (youtube, instagram, twitter, etc.)

On the other end of the spectrum, companies which already have expertise in producing online-first content (e.g. Google) are now entering the realm of cultural institutions in order to gain credibility out of the cultural capital invested in such institutions.

outro

reproduction is necessary for art and democracy.

the approach has been to focus on visitors and reach them in their online worlds rather than create their own.

While reproduction seemed to be a problem at first, it's actually a democratic necessity. the second problem after that is to build the interface which allows you to interact with those reproductions. As usual, this is modulated by visitor behaviour, which leads us today, to integration with social media platform and integration into the life of the user by bringing them back always to the museum.
