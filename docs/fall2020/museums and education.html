mediation and social distinctions

the modern museum is stretched between an aesthetic (learning about perspective in italy, or about enluminure, fauvism, impressionism), ethical (war museums, holocaust memorials) and social (futurium: living together in the future, museums for engineering, museum for german history, DDR museum, national museum of X ) role.

so the question is: how can someone develop knowledge just by looking at something?

in needs to be in context/background knowledge (collections, texts)

Mediation means meeting someone in the middle, of having to concile (or re-concile!) two or more different parties. What is reconciled here is the truth held by museums, as the cultural heritage which needs to be transmitted to the public in order to reinforce the sense of unity and communal belonging of this public. Additionally, mediation and education are rooted in the enlightenment spirit of learning and developing knowledge so that, in the end, they transmit their worldview to the audience.

The questions here are those of understanding and seeing, objectivity and subjectivity, externalising and internalising: what are some ways of transmitting, and facilitating the development of ideas, concepts and knowledge.

But if mediation—and its counterpart, interpretation—imply closing the gap on the distance between the artwork and the public, wouldn't be interesting to know why that gap is here in the first place?

hermeneutics

the methodology of interpreting sacred texts reinforces the sacredness of these texts.

Museums have, for a long time, never questioned how to proceed with education. It has always been top-down, explanatory, or even ommitted. Mediation has indeed been activated once the audience is in the museum, and not so much before such audiences would come to the museum: the assumption here is that everyone in the museum comes for the same reasons, wants to learn the same thing.

The approach is therefore self-contained and self-referential: it only exists within the world of the museum, since the museum represents the world, and it explains the artworks only in relationship to the artworks themselves. Art for art's sake is the manifestation of the luxury/privilege.

taste as distinction

the sacredness implies some sort of hierarchy

"museums exists so that people who go to museums can distinguish themselves from those who do not go" - pierre bourdieu

"the museum as trend"

taste is socially constructed, but it still possibly individually defined—by experiencing more things, being more exposed, learning/reading about it at the same time, paying attention and motivations.

high-art is not a necessity, "pure" gaze, and self-referential

process of coding, and decoding, and "knowing what something means"

  • social capital
  • economic capital
  • cultural capital
  • symbolic capital

Bourdieu has a very strict sense of culture: it is culture as an economic product, a market in which value is produced, exchanged and kept in order to maintain relationships of power. He places the museum in this dynamic as a symbolic way to separate between those who know and those who don't know; it also makes the distinction between those who need to learn, and those who don't need to learn (because they come from distinctive social classes).

classical approach

the teacher provides knowledge to the learner by feeding them truths/facts that already exist about the world. (formative, logical, efficient; risk of being boring and passive; depends on what you're being taught-foundational knowledge)

The content isn't interactive, very well-organized and has clear learning outcomes.

constructivism

any experience which allows for subsequent, novel experiences is a learning experience + learning by doing

Constructivism has a particular appeal to the museum, because it is suited to informal, voluntary, one-shot nature of most learning which happens in a museum.

First developed by John Dewey, political philosopher and educator, constructivism challenges the passive learner approach and offers activities to the learner so that they can construct knowledge by themselves.

Discovery learning, an offshoot, still involves teacher supervision, assessment/feedback and example solutions, and also requires pre-exisiting knowledge. In order to build from pre-existing knowledge, it therefore becomes important to know the background of your visitors/students/participants.

new skills

"A teacher tells, a facilitator asks; a teacher lectures from the front, a facilitator supports from the back; a teacher gives answers according to a set curriculum, a facilitator provides guidelines and creates the environment for the learner to arrive at his or her own conclusions; a teacher mostly gives a monologue, a facilitator is in continuous dialogue with the learners." - wikipedia

teaching to transgress

the most important question in education is:

why should one learn this? depends more and more on the background of the visitor (personal trajectories, and where they come from)

once this question is answered, the following is:

how should one learn this?

All of the above are part of a broader field named critical pedagogies, including educators such as paulo freire, who brings in ideas about narration and storytelling as essential to learning and bell hooks, who argues that a central element of teaching is the ability for the teacher to encourage and motivate individuals into a learning situation through performance (i.e. not just reading notes from a pedestal in an amphitheater).


museum initiatives

museum responses

while the goal remain the same, the means to achieve those goals have evolved.

  • paper
  • audio
  • tours
  • workshops
  • collaborations
  • summer camps for children/teens/adults / class visits
  • challenges and competitions
  • family programs

Museums have educational activities through:

  • information labels
  • gallery tours
  • informal gallery presence
  • class visits
  • family programs
  • organizational partnerships
  • online educational programs
  • discussions and panels

Amongst these, the first ones on the list (labels and tours) are more didactic and unidirectional, while the others can be developed into more participative and horizontal practices.

The later items (family activities, online programs) present the added complication that learning in museums is conducted in conditions ill-suited to traditional learning: short duration, sporadic, in unfamiliar settings and unrelated to existing disciplined educational exposure. This, however, does make them more conducive to a constructivist approach to education.

collaborating physically

physical activities can be the starting point for any sort of learning experience.

Museums invite more and more artist collectives to create experiences in their physical spaces, which involve the audience. The TLC collective in Mexico City develops activities with the museum visitors, and start by asking questions in order to understand what is it that is interesting to them.

Complementary to this face-to-face approach, the architecture agency raumlabor has partnered with the staatliches museen zu berlin in order to install interactive installations which involve the visitors further with the artworks they are currently seeing in the room. (full 360 visit here)
