past and present

pre-bourgeoisie galleries

galleries are essentially the same as the museum

Originally, the difference between the museum and the gallery didn't exist. It is with the emergence of the bourgeois class, with enough money to comission and purchase artworks that the gallery, an independent space without historical, royal or political credentials, started to open.

It's also worth noting that "traditional" galleries (i.e., museums) did not always, and still do not, indulge into what we see today as the "white cube".

modernism

modernism is a new version of "art for art's sake", with clemens greenberg at the forefront.

This is accompanied by, at the turn of the twentietch century, the emergence of modernism. This is more of a theoretical component, and this implies that the space the art is shown in should be first and foremost dedicated to the unique appreciation of art.

the art market

the shift starts at the end of the 19th century, as artists start exhibiting works with private dealers.

As art dealers appear (this guy here is one of the first ones, Durand-Ruel in France), the collection of these commodities influence how we view them (i.e. "the hype" of the artist).

this can also lead to a clear "instrumentalization" of art by the galleries (and perhaps even by government, as the CIA did with the abstract expressionists).

while museums don't want to get involved with contemporary production (they still do get involved with contemporary discussions), art galleries are very happy to make or break artists.

the curator

she acts as the maker of meaning, along with the collector, the dealer and the critic.

This rise of the commercial art gallery also means that there is a new need for organizing shows, for exhibition-making. This figure of the curator becomes in charge of the narrative given to the works, of how we understand it.

There is also a tension between the "art for itself" and the two roles that emerge: curator and critic. On the one hand, the work is "self-explanatory", on the other hand, it needs to be justified by people in order to fit within the canon of already accepted artworks.

reconnecting to museums

private foundations are behaving more and more like museums, while keeping an active influence on the art market.

So the state of things now is that of a highly influenced art world by the commercial structures that are set up to exchange art for monetary value, and are therefore not bound to "tradition", "heritage" or "education", as more classical museum institutions are.

Particularly, the emergence of incredibly large galleries (e.g. Gagosian, David Zwirner), as well as international art fairs (Basel, Paris Biennale, Frieze), both enable and stifle innovation, the same way that a capitalistic market favors both outsiders and monopolies.


the art experiments

the white cube

the gallery as a white cube starts as a decontextualization of the space in favor of the art.

seeing art

this setup implies that the only thing that matters in art is seeing

The eye becomes something that is both personal (vision is subjective, individual) and at the same time de-humanizing (it is the only sense, the only part of the body that is allowed to be activated).

room to play with

breaking away from the restriction of the blank space, the installation comes to life.

Parallel to the development of the white cube, comes the installation. Since the gallery becomes a sacred space exclusively devoted to art, then it actually follows that art should be able to do whatever it wants from it, to get away from the wall and invade the whole space.

This starts a new trend of experimentation in art: starting from constraints, the artists push back and explore new possibilities (Sol Lewitt, Richard Serra, James Turrell).

More than simply a sculpture, an installation uses the whole space to integrate the body of the visitor.

making art

galleries both maintain status quo and are a space for experimentations


digital in galleries

digital media in galleries

the art-focused approach of galleries leaves to approaches to digital media:

  • developing it around the gallery activity - peripheral
  • developing it around the artistic activity - integral

The activity of gallery is therefore not so much about educating the public about art and culture, but rather to move what makes up a part of art and culture in a particular direction (e.g. new artistic tendencies and movements). The role of digital media as we've seen it so far in museums (educational) is less relevant in galleries, and therefore should be rethought.

It would either go in a completely profane direction (i.e. having not relationship to the art itself, but rather to the activity of the gallery), or integrated the "sacred" nature of the artwork (i.e. become an artwork itself).

outro

conclusion

the unique role of art galleries is pushing AR not so much as an informative medium, but as an artistic medium in its own right.

on the one hand, art galleries are tied to the market, and therefore influence the present, by maintaining the reputation of established artists, or by introducing new artists.

on the other hand, the limitations of the white cube, the desire for innovation, and the emergence of artist-run galleries have brough about more innovation.

as such, digital media isn't so much an informative medium than an artistic medium.
